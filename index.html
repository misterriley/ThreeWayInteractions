Analysis Pipeline & Methodology

                            
Detailed mathematical steps used to resolve connectivity and three-way interactions

                        

                        

                            

                                
1. Spatial Separation & Canonical Network Averaging

                                
Timeseries signals are initially extracted from 268 region-of-interest (ROI) nodes across the brain. To reduce dimensionality and focus on systems-level communication, the nodes are mapped to 10 canonical networks defined by functional atlases:

                                
    
                                    
  • DMN: Default Mode Network
    • 
                                    
  • SC: Subcortical
    • 
                                    
  • FP: Frontoparietal Control
    • 
                                    
  • Mot: Motor
    • 
                                    
  • MF: Medial Frontal
    • 
                                    
  • VAs: Visual Association
    • 
                                    
  • VI: Visual I
    • 
                                    
  • VII: Visual II
    • 
                                    
  • SAL: Salience
    • 
                                    
  • CBL: Cerebellar
    • 
                                

                                
For each participant, timeseries are first normalized (z-scored over space at each timepoint) and then normalized (z-scored over time at each node). The spatial averaging is performed by taking the mean timeseries across all ROIs belonging to each network, resulting in a 10 × T timeseries representation.

                            


                            

                                
2. Individual Functional Connectivity (FC)

                                
Pearson correlation is calculated on the 10 × T network-averaged timeseries for each subject, generating a symmetric positive-semidefinite 10 × 10 correlation matrix representing their pairwise functional connectivity:

                                

                                    $$\mathbf{C}_i = \text{corr}(\mathbf{Y}_i^T)$$
                                

                            


                            

                                
3. Bures-Wasserstein Fréchet Mean (Barycenter)

                                
Rather than using a simple element-wise arithmetic mean of correlation matrices—which washes out individual phase boundaries and boundaries on the PSD manifold—we compute the group-level baseline as the Fréchet mean under the Bures-Wasserstein Riemannian metric. This is solved via the Alvarez-Esteban fixed-point iteration:

                                

                                    $$\mathbf{S}_{k+1} = \mathbf{S}_k^{-1/2} \left( \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N (\mathbf{S}_k^{1/2} \mathbf{C}_i \mathbf{S}_k^{1/2})^{1/2} \right)^2 \mathbf{S}_k^{-1/2}$$
                                

                                
The converged covariance barycenter $\mathbf{S}$ is normalized to yield a true correlation matrix $\bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}$. To ensure that only stable, statistically sound connections are visualized, we run 100 bootstrap iterations, compute the standard deviation of each connection, convert them to Z-scores (Bootstrap Ratios), and apply a Bonferroni-corrected Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER) threshold ($\alpha = 0.05$ across 45 comparisons):

                                

                                    $$Z_{\text{threshold}} = \sqrt{2} \text{erf}^{-1}(1 - \frac{0.05}{45}) \approx 3.29$$
                                

                            


                            

                                
4. Principal Geodesic Analysis (PGA)

                                
PGA extends PCA to Riemannian manifolds. We map each subject's functional connectivity matrix $\mathbf{C}_i$ to the tangent space at the Bures-Wasserstein barycenter $\bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}$ via the logarithmic map:

                                

                                    $$\mathbf{X}_i = (\bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}^{1/2} \mathbf{C}_i \bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}^{1/2})^{1/2} - \bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}$$
                                

                                
To preserve the Riemannian metric, the symmetric tangent matrices $\mathbf{X}_i$ are vectorized into 55-dimensional vectors with off-diagonal entries scaled by $\sqrt{2}$. We perform standard PCA on these tangent vectors to extract principal components $\mathbf{u}_k$ (geodesic loadings). A bootstrap resampling (100 runs) and Bonferroni FWER threshold ($\alpha = 0.05$ across 55 features) are applied to yield sparse, stable loadings. The geodesic variations are reconstructed along each PC direction using the exponential map:

                                

                                    $$\mathbf{C}_{\text{rec}}(t) = (\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{Z}_t) \bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}} (\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{Z}_t), \quad \text{where } \mathbf{Z}_t = t \cdot \bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}^{-1/2} \mathbf{X}_{\text{pc}} \bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}^{-1/2}$$
                                

                            


                            

                                
5. Group Symmetric CP Tensor Decomposition

                                
To characterize three-way nonlinear dynamics, we compute the third-order coskewness tensor (10 × 10 × 10) for each subject:

                                

                                    $$\mathcal{T}_{ijk}^{(s)} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T y_{i,t} \cdot y_{j,t} \cdot y_{k,t}$$
                                

                                
To identify shared components across the cohort, we stack all subject tensors into a 4D array $\mathcal{X}$ of size 10 × 10 × 10 × N and perform a group-level symmetric Canonical Polyadic (CP) decomposition using Alternating Least Squares (ALS) with $R = 5$ latent modes, enforcing symmetry on the first three spatial modes:

                                

                                    $$\mathcal{X}_{ijks} \approx \sum_{r=1}^{R} \lambda_r \cdot v_{r,i} \cdot v_{r,j} \cdot v_{r,k} \cdot w_{r,s}$$
                                

                                
This resolves spatial modes $\mathbf{v}_r \in \mathbb{R}^{10}$, cohort loading scores $\mathbf{w}_r \in \mathbb{R}^N$, and mode weights $\lambda_r$. We reconstruct the population target tensor as: $\mathcal{W}_{ijk} = \sum_{r=1}^{R} \lambda_r v_{r,i} v_{r,j} v_{r,k}$.

                            


                            

                                
6. De-whitening & Precision-Matrix Contraction (Adjusted Triad Index $M_{abc}$)

                                
The raw third-order moments contain significant lower-order linear correlation dependencies. To isolate pure three-way nonlinear interactions, we filter the population tensor $\mathcal{W}$ by contracting it with the precision matrix $\mathbf{\Theta} = \bar{\mathbf{\Sigma}}^{-1}$ (the inverse of the Wasserstein barycenter baseline):

                                

                                    $$M_{abc} = \sum_{i=1}^{10} \sum_{j=1}^{10} \sum_{k=1}^{10} \mathcal{W}_{ijk} \cdot \mathbf{\Theta}_{ia} \cdot \mathbf{\Theta}_{jb} \cdot \mathbf{\Theta}_{kc}$$
                                

                                
This contraction mathematically represents a de-whitening transformation. By projecting the joint third-order moments through the inverse covariance, we remove lower-order Gaussian correlation effects. This is mathematically equivalent to computing the expectation of multivariate **Hermite polynomials** of degree 3, which are orthogonal expansions that adjust skewness for the covariance structure, ensuring that only pure, non-linear three-way modulations are ranked.
